PROCESS

From raw cotton to finished cloth — the steps that make a handloom saree.

HOW IT IS MADE

01

YARN PREPARATION

Raw yarn arrives at the cooperative and is wound onto bobbins using warping boards. The thread count and colour are selected according to the order. For Kasavu sarees, a separate bobbin of zari (gold-wrapped thread) is prepared.

02

WARPING

The warp — the set of longitudinal threads — is wound onto the warp beam of the loom. Each thread is counted and measured to the exact length of the finished saree. A standard Kerala saree requires between 4,000 and 6,000 warp threads.

03

DRESSING THE LOOM

Each warp thread is individually threaded through the heddle eyes and then through the reed (a comb-like device that spaces the threads evenly). This process, called sleying, can take an entire day for a single setup.

04

WEAVING

The weaver sits at the loom and passes the weft shuttle through the shed — the opening created when alternate warp threads are lifted by the harness. The beater presses each pick (row) into the cloth. A skilled weaver at Eravathody produces between 3 and 5 metres of cloth per day.

05

KASAVU BORDER

When weaving the Kasavu border, a second shuttle carrying zari thread is introduced. The weaver alternates between the plain weft and the zari, creating the characteristic golden band. Complex border patterns require the weaver to follow a mental draft, counting each sequence.

06

FINISHING

The finished cloth is cut from the loom, washed to remove sizing, lightly starched, and dried in open air. The saree is then inspected, folded in the traditional manner, and certified with the Handloom Mark before leaving the cooperative.
